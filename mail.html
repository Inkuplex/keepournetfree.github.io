Dear [Your House Representative],

The Senate has recently passed a resolution that puts the privacy of all U.S. internet users at risk. The bill in question, S.J. Res. 34, kills an FCC regulation passed last December requiring telecommunications carriers to, among several other things, provide heightened notice and obtain affirmative consent from customers when offering financial incentives in exchange for said customers' information. The resolution will soon be entering the House as H.J. Res. 86. If it passes, this as well as several other key regulations will not take effect.

Information trading is nothing new. Companies like Google and Facebook use it to provide ads targeted to the preferences of their users. The issue, however, lies in the fact that if this rule and the others in this bill were killed, it would also allow Internet Service Providers (ISPs) to participate in information trading without their customers' consent - and whereas Facebook's information trading can be avoided by simply not using Facebook, it is nigh-impossible for U.S. citizens to avoid using the Internet. On top of this, many regions of the U.S. are under a near-monopoly when it comes to choosing their ISP. Without these rules, there would be very little the average U.S. citizen would be able to do to avoid having their information leveraged for the financial gain of their ISP, at their expense. In addition, once our information is sold, there is no telling what can be done with it. Whether you use the internet for research, work, social media, entertainment, or pornography, anyone can pay to know in order to leverage whatever information they find against you. The potential damage that may stem from this possibility is vast beyond measure.

As a concerned citizen and constituent, I urge you to vote no on H.J. Res. 86, and protect the privacy rights of all citizens. Thank you in advance for your consideration.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]
